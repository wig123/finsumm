[Data Relationships]
- 1980: Investment 33%–36%; Household 48%–52%; Government 10%–14%; Net exports 2%–4%.
- 2010: Investment 45%–50%; Household 32%–38%; Government 12%–16%; Net exports 3%–6%.
- 2020: Investment 40%–44%; Household 38%–42%; Government 12%–16%; Net exports 4%–6%.
- Net exports are consistently <10% across all years; Government + Net exports combined total approximately 15%–25%.
- Investment in 2010 was approximately 10–15 percentage points higher than in 1980; and approximately 3–8 percentage points higher than in 2020.

[Pattern Characteristics]
- The composition across all years is dominated by Investment and Household expenditure proportions, while Government and Net exports proportions are smaller and show limited fluctuation.
- The Investment proportion first rises and then converges over the long cycle; Household expenditure shows an inverse trend, with the disparity between the two being more noticeable around 2010.
- Investment was at a relatively low level around 2000 and a relatively high level around 2010; Net exports consistently maintain a low level.

[Core Insights]
Core Conclusion: Aggregate composition relies on investment, while consumption contribution is relatively weak.

Business Implications:
- Demand structure is more reliant on investment
- Consumption contribution to the aggregate total is relatively low
- Fiscal policy and foreign trade have limited impact on the structure

Risk Concerns:
- Investment proportion ≥50%: Consumption share is compressed, leading to excessive structural concentration
- Government + Net exports ≥25%: Dominated by policy and external demand, reducing flexibility
