[Pattern Characteristics]
- The overall trends of the two series were relatively similar between 1950 and 2000, with localized synchronous peaks and decline intervals, though the magnitudes differed.
- After approximately 2002, the PAT margin showed a stepwise increase and fluctuated near high levels, while Wages-to-income first declined significantly before recovering. The gap between the two widened substantially during this period.
- Around 2008–2012, Wages-to-income showed a clear trough at a low level, while the PAT margin did not experience a corresponding degree of decline during the same period.

[Core Insights]
Core Conclusion: After 2002, the profit proportion and the wage proportion exhibited a continuously widening structural divergence.

Business Implications:
- The structural dependence of corporate profitability on factor distribution has changed.
- The correlation between the income statement and compensation costs has significantly weakened.
- The sensitivity of labor compensation to changes in overall earnings has decreased.

Risk Concerns:
- If the PAT margin consistently remains above 12% while Wages-to-income is below 43%, it may amplify conflicts over benefit distribution.
- If Wages-to-income rises above 47% while the PAT margin falls below 8%, it could compress corporate profit margins and affect reinvestment.
