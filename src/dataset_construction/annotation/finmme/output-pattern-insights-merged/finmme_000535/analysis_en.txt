[Pattern Characteristics]
- The performance of each framework across the six characteristics shows clear stratification: most frameworks feature bidirectional coverage in both assessment and disclosure dimensions, while a few cover only one direction or are optional.
- Regarding the two structural characteristics, value chain and location information, most frameworks perform consistently, with only individual frameworks showing flexibility or temporary non-coverage in downstream coverage and location usage.
- GRI and SBTN exhibit similar localized gaps in the assessment and disclosure dimensions related to dependencies, risks, and opportunities, while TNFD, ESRS, and ISSB show a highly uniform and consistent structure across multiple characteristics.

[Core Insights]
Core Conclusion: Frameworks show clear stratification in assessment and disclosure completeness, indicating significant differences in standard maturity.

Business Implications:
- TNFD, ESRS, and ISSB facilitate the construction of relatively complete nature-related management systems.
- Adopting GRI or SBTN easily leads to insufficient dependency and risk identification and information transmission.
- The Natural Capital Protocol is suitable for flexible application, but its comparability and mandatory degree are limited.

Risk Focus:
- Relying solely on frameworks that do not cover "Assessment of nature-related risks and opportunities" (such as GRI, SBTN) may lead to failure to identify major nature-related risks, affecting capital allocation.
- If a framework with "optional disclosure" is used to handle dependencies and impacts (such as the Natural Capital Protocol), a disclosure proportion below 100% may weaken investor and regulatory trust.
